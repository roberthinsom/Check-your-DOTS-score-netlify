Check Your DOTS Score: The Ultimate Tool for Powerlifting Performance

What is the DOTS Score?

The DOTS score is a performance metric used in powerlifting that standardizes an athlete's performance based on their body weight and lifting totals. Developed by Dr. Brandon D. K. Turner, the DOTS system is designed to help powerlifters of different body weights and experience levels compare their performance more fairly. It provides a comprehensive look at how well an athlete performs relative to their body weight, giving more context to their raw total.

Unlike other scoring methods, such as Wilks, which have limitations when comparing athletes across different weight classes, DOTS aims to provide a more consistent, universal standard for performance evaluation. It takes into account an athlete’s body weight, ensuring that lighter lifters are not disadvantaged when compared to heavier lifters.

DOTS Formula

The DOTS score is calculated using a proprietary formula that combines an athlete’s total (the sum of their best squat, bench press, and deadlift) with their body weight. The formula is mathematically designed to adjust for the variance in body mass across different athletes, ultimately providing a normalized score that allows for more accurate cross-class comparisons.

Why Should Powerlifters Check Their DOTS Score?

1. Objective Performance Evaluation

The DOTS score removes the subjective bias that often comes with raw totals and weight class comparisons. By adjusting for body weight, the DOTS score provides a level playing field, enabling athletes to compare their performances against others of varying body sizes. When you check your DOTS score, you'll get an unbiased perspective on your progress, which is crucial for understanding your true performance.

2. Tracking Progress Over Time

One of the most challenging aspects of powerlifting is tracking long-term progress. Powerlifters often become fixated on increasing their raw totals, but the DOTS score helps put those numbers into context. By regularly checking your DOTS score, you can better assess your strength development in relation to your body weight, ensuring that your progress is moving in the right direction. A stagnant raw total might seem discouraging, but if your DOTS score is increasing, that means you're improving.

3. Setting Realistic Goals

Knowing your DOTS score can be instrumental when setting goals. For example, if your current DOTS score is below the average for athletes in your weight class or skill level, you can use this information to set more realistic and structured goals. Instead of just focusing on adding more weight to your lifts, you can strive for a specific improvement in your DOTS score, providing you with a more quantifiable and clear target.

Understanding How the DOTS Score Affects Your Performance

1. Body Weight and Strength Balance

Strength-to-weight ratio is a crucial element in powerlifting. The DOTS score adjusts for body weight, giving you an idea of how well you lift relative to your size. Athletes who can lift significantly more than their body weight will naturally have higher DOTS scores. For instance, if two lifters each total 1,000 pounds, but one weighs 165 pounds and the other weighs 220 pounds, the lighter lifter would have a higher DOTS score because they are lifting more relative to their body weight.

2. Encouraging a Holistic Approach

By factoring in both body weight and total lifts, the DOTS system encourages a more holistic approach to training. Instead of obsessing over only increasing the total weight lifted, lifters are prompted to focus on achieving the best balance between strength and body weight. This can help prevent extreme weight loss or gain just to meet specific total goals, which may lead to injury or burnout in the long run.

Comparing the DOTS Score to Other Powerlifting Scoring Systems

1. DOTS vs. Wilks Score

The Wilks score is another popular scoring system used in powerlifting that adjusts performance based on body weight. However, while the Wilks formula has been widely accepted for many years, it has its drawbacks, especially when comparing athletes from different weight classes. The Wilks score tends to favor larger lifters due to its underlying coefficients, which often give less accurate results for lighter lifters.

In contrast, the DOTS system offers a more balanced approach, providing a more accurate reflection of an athlete’s performance regardless of size. The DOTS score is calculated using a more modern statistical method that accounts for various factors in a way that’s fairer and more inclusive of all body types.

2. DOTS vs. IPF Points

The International Powerlifting Federation (IPF) points system is another scoring method used in powerlifting competitions. Similar to Wilks, the IPF points system is also designed to account for body weight, but it uses a different set of coefficients and scoring criteria. While both IPF points and DOTS aim to standardize performance across weight classes, DOTS is generally considered a more refined and accurate tool for cross-comparison, especially when it comes to evaluating athletes of diverse body types.

How to Improve Your DOTS Score

Improving your DOTS score requires a strategic approach to both strength training and body weight management. Here are some key tips:

1. Focus on Strength Gains

The most straightforward way to increase your DOTS score is to increase your lifting totals. Regularly training your squat, bench press, and deadlift with appropriate periodization, progressive overload, and recovery strategies will help you build strength over time. Focusing on compound movements and addressing weak points in your lifts can significantly contribute to improvements in your overall total.

2. Maintain an Optimal Body Weight

Maintaining an optimal body weight is crucial for improving your DOTS score. While it may be tempting to cut weight to compete in a lighter class, this can sometimes result in a loss of strength. Instead, focus on staying within a healthy body weight range that allows you to perform your best lifts without excessive weight fluctuations. Keeping your body fat percentage in check while building lean muscle will help you maximize your strength-to-weight ratio.

3. Train for Technique and Efficiency

Maximizing technique is as important as lifting heavy weights. Improving your lifting technique for each of the three powerlifts ensures you’re using your body most efficiently. Small adjustments in form can lead to significant improvements in the amount of weight you can lift. Consider working with a coach or analyzing videos of your lifts to identify areas for improvement.
